The Problem

Three Joints. One Pattern. One Fix.

Every cyclist has a weak link. Most have three. Here's what's happening inside your body on every ride.

1
🦵 The Knee
IT Band & Patellar Tracking

What Happens

Your knee flexes and extends roughly 10,000 times per hour on the bike. With every revolution, the IT band and patellar tendon absorb stress that your glutes and hip stabilizers should be managing.

Why Cycling Makes It Worse

The fixed pedaling motion trains your knee to track in one plane — but imbalanced supporting muscles let it drift inward, creating friction and inflammation with every stroke.

⚠ Warning Signs You're There

  • Clicking or popping on the outside of the knee
  • Sharp pain on the lateral knee, especially after hour 1
  • Pain going downstairs or squatting the day after a ride
2
🏃 The Hip
Hip Flexor & Psoas Tightness

What Happens

Spending hours bent at 90° causes your hip flexors — especially the psoas — to shorten and tighten. This creates anterior pelvic tilt: your pelvis tips forward, compressing your lower back and reducing power transfer.

Why Cycling Makes It Worse

Unlike running, cycling never takes the hip through full extension. The hip flexors are perpetually contracted and never fully lengthened, training them to stay short even off the bike.

⚠ Warning Signs You're There

  • Tightness at the top of the pedal stroke (pulling up feels restricted)
  • Low back ache that starts after 45+ minutes in the saddle
  • Hip "impingement" feeling when climbing steep grades
3
🔙 The Lower Back
Lumbar Compression & Glute Shutdown

What Happens

In the aero position, your lumbar spine rounds into sustained flexion. Combined with glutes that have essentially shut off, your lower back muscles compensate by overworking — leading to chronic compression and fatigue.

Why Cycling Makes It Worse

Weak glutes mean your back tries to do the power generation work instead. The more hours you log, the more ingrained this compensation pattern becomes — until it becomes your permanent movement default.

⚠ Warning Signs You're There

  • Stiffness getting off the bike that takes 10+ minutes to ease
  • Back pain that progressively worsens over a long ride
  • Feeling like you "sit into" your back rather than engaging your core
The Root Cause

It's Not the Miles. It's the Pattern.

All three problems trace back to the same root cause — and it's not what most cyclists think.

Your body adapts to what you do most. If you're on a bike 5–10 hours a week, it adapts to that position. Certain muscles become dominant and overactive. Others become weak and inhibited. This creates muscle imbalance — and imbalanced muscles put joints under stress they were never designed to handle.


The cycling position is biomechanically efficient for producing power — but it's also one of the most one-sided, repetitive movement patterns in sport. Without targeted intervention, the body breaks down. Every time.
