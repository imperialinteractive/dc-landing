Dynamic Cyclist

I Was About to Quit Cycling at 52.

— Mike Dearing, Penticton BC
[Photo: Mike on his road bike, Okanagan valley backdrop, summer morning light]

I've been riding for 22 years. I know what tired legs feel like. I know what bonking feels like. I know what it feels like to push too hard and pay for it the next day. This was different.

Every ride over an hour, my lower back would seize up so badly I'd have to stop and stretch on the side of the road. Not occasionally. Every time. At the 90-minute mark, reliably, I'd feel it starting — a tightening across the lumbar, then a pulling down through my left hip, then a point where I was no longer really riding so much as managing. Managing the pain, managing the position, trying to find the angle that kept it tolerable.

I'd tried everything.

Three different physios over two years, all of whom helped temporarily and none of whom gave me anything that lasted. A professional bike fit — a good one, expensive, thorough — which got me six weeks of relief before things crept back to where they'd been. A $400 saddle because someone in my club swore by it. Every YouTube stretch video with "cyclists" in the title. Hot yoga twice a week for a month. Ice, heat, compression, anti-inflammatories.

Nothing worked for more than a week.

In October last year, I did a 4-hour charity ride I'd done eight times before. The Fondueuse loop — beautiful route through the valley, not particularly hard by anyone's measure. I've done it in training condition and I've done it undertrained. I know that ride.

I had to stop at 2.5 hours. I pulled off at a rest stop, sat down on a bench, and genuinely wondered if I was done. Not done with the ride. Done with cycling. The thing I've organised my weekends around for two decades, the thing I still look forward to every Tuesday and Thursday morning regardless of what's going on at work — I sat there at 2.5 hours into a 4-hour ride and thought: maybe this is just over.

"I sat on a bench and genuinely wondered if I was done."

A few weeks later, a guy in my cycling club — Paul, retired physiotherapist, rides with us on Saturdays — mentioned something called Dynamic Cyclist. He'd been using it for about three months and said it was the first thing that had addressed what he called "the structural problem underneath the cycling problem."

I was sceptical. It looked like another generic stretch program when I first pulled it up. I almost didn't start it. The sessions were shorter than I expected — 15 to 20 minutes — which made me doubt it would do anything meaningful. I wanted something that felt like work. This felt almost too simple.

The first 10 days, I noticed nothing. I kept riding. I kept stopping. I kept stretching on the side of the road. I nearly quit the program, which would have been a significant mistake.

On day 12 — I remember this specifically because I wrote it down — I did my usual Tuesday 90-minute loop and arrived back at the car and realised I hadn't checked how my back was feeling. Because it hadn't been an issue. It was just a ride. Normal. The thing I hadn't had in two years: a completely unremarkable 90-minute ride where my back was just a part of my body doing its job rather than the thing I was managing the whole time.

Six weeks in, I went back and did the Fondueuse loop. Four hours, same route. I finished it. Rode back to the car, loaded the bike, drove home, made dinner. My wife asked how it went. I said fine. And then I went to the bathroom because I didn't want her to see me getting emotional about riding a bike for four hours like a normal person.

"I cried a bit on the last climb. My wife thought I was being dramatic."

What the program actually is: it's not yoga. It's not physiotherapy. It's 15–20 minutes every morning, built specifically for people who ride bikes. The sessions work through the specific compensation patterns that cycling creates — the tight hip flexors, the inhibited glutes, the lumbar overload — in a specific sequence. The sequence matters. That's what makes it different from everything else I tried. The other things I tried treated the symptoms. This treats the pattern that's creating the symptoms.

I ride five days a week now. Did my first century last month, which I hadn't attempted in four years because I knew I'd have to stop somewhere after 80km. I didn't stop. I felt better at kilometre 140 than I used to feel at kilometre 60.

I'm 52. I plan to still be doing this at 62. That felt uncertain six months ago. It doesn't anymore.

If you're anywhere near where I was — managing the ride instead of riding it, wondering if this is just what getting older feels like, trying things that don't last — just try the 7 days free. You've got nothing to lose except the thing that's been slowing you down.
