Hey —

My physio said something to me last Tuesday that I haven't been able to stop thinking about.

I'm 49. Been riding for 12 years. I'm not in pain exactly — I ride fine, I finish my rides, I don't have any one injury that would make you feel sorry for me. But I've also never felt quite right on the bike. There's always something. A tightness. A thing I'm managing. A part of the ride where I stop enjoying it and start enduring it.

I'd stopped noticing, honestly. I thought that was just what cycling felt like for someone my age.

Anyway. Last Tuesday. I walked into the clinic and my physio — I've been seeing her on and off for three years — watched me walk across the room and said, before I'd even sat down:

"Your engine is turned off."

I asked what that meant. She explained Lower Crossed Syndrome. I'd never heard the term. Here's what it is:

Your body has an X-pattern of tight and weak muscles that runs across your pelvis. Tight hip flexors and tight lower back. Weak glutes and weak core. They pull against each other diagonally — hence the X. In that configuration, the muscles that should be generating power in your pedal stroke have essentially gone offline. They're not firing. They've been replaced by smaller, less capable muscles that are doing a job they were never designed for.

Cycling doesn't cause this, exactly. But it accelerates it dramatically. Every hour in the saddle — hips flexed, back rounded, glutes disengaged — reinforces the pattern. The hip flexors shorten. The glutes inhibit. The lower back compensates. Round and round.

I asked her how long she thought I'd had it.

"Probably five or six years," she said. "Your body has just been compensating for it the whole time."

Five or six years. Every ride for five or six years, my body routing around a fundamental dysfunction I had no idea was there.

I went home and looked for something designed specifically for cyclists. Not a generic flexibility program. Not a yoga app. Something that understood the pattern she was describing and worked through it systematically.

I tried two things that didn't address it — they were just generic stretching with a cycling photo on the thumbnail. Then I found Dynamic Cyclist. The way they described it matched exactly what she'd said about the sequence mattering — you have to release the tight tissues before you can activate the weak ones, or you're just spinning wheels.

Three weeks in, I did my usual long Saturday ride. 110km, nothing special. I got back to the car and realised the back hadn't become the story. The whole ride, it was just a ride. I hadn't spent the last 40km managing. I'd just ridden.

Small thing, maybe. But it's been three years since I had a long ride where that was true.
